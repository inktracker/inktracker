## Ink Types & Chemistry

### Plastisol (PVC-Based) – The Workhorse

**Overview:** Plastisol is the dominant textile screen printing ink. It's a suspension of PVC particles in plasticizer (dioctyl phthalate or similar). It requires heat—not solvent or air—to cure, making it ideal for production apparel.

**Composition & Properties:**
- Thick, creamy consistency
- Ships in plastic containers; stays liquid at room temperature (store below 90°F to prevent premature cure)
- Opaque, covers dark garments well
- Good color saturation; available in 1000+ shades
- Standard cure temperature: 320°F (160°C) at the ink core, with 90–120 seconds of dwell time
- Low-cure variants available at 260–280°F to prevent dye migration on polyester

**Pros:**
- Longest shelf life when stored properly
- Exceptional color range and opacity
- Works on nearly all fabrics (cotton, cotton blends, polyester, synthetics)
- Minimal learning curve for new printers
- Excellent for halftone and detail work when paired with proper mesh

**Cons:**
- Creates a thick hand (feel) on fabric; prints sit on top of the garment
- Plastisol may not fully cure on dark polyester without specialty inks
- Requires heat curing equipment (dryer belt or flash unit)
- Environmental concerns around PVC and phthalate plasticizers (some manufacturers now offer phthalate-free formulas)
- Not washable until fully cured; undercuts and gel-cure stages require strict temperature control

**Viscosity:** Fresh plastisol from the jug is typically 95,000–150,000 cPs (centipoise). Higher viscosity = thicker ink = more opaque but slower flow through mesh. Lower viscosity = thinner ink = finer detail but less coverage. Printers often cut plastisol with reducing agent (3–5% by weight) to lower viscosity for finer halftones or detail work.

### Water-Based Inks – Soft Hand & Breathable

**Overview:** Water-based inks dry by absorption into the fabric and evaporation of water. They produce a genuinely soft hand and are the green choice for apparel printing.

**Properties:**
- Thinner consistency than plastisol; water-soluble carrier
- Soft, breathable prints that feel like part of the garment
- Lower opacity than plastisol; may require multiple passes on dark garments
- Cure via air dry (24–48 hours) or forced-air dry (3–5 minutes at 160–180°F)
- Absorb into fabric fibers; not a film on the surface

**Pros:**
- Genuinely soft hand feel; best for fashion and performance wear
- Environmentally friendlier (no phthalates, water-soluble)
- Lower heat requirement for cure
- Can print fine halftones (requires high mesh: 200–305)

**Cons:**
- Poor opacity on dark garments; struggles on black or navy without a white underbase
- Requires higher mesh counts (180–230+) to prevent flooding
- Slower curing than plastisol (air dry = overnight delays)
- Not as color-saturated as plastisol
- Reclaiming screens is tricky; residue can linger
- More expensive per ounce than plastisol
- Challenges on polyester; washes off rather than adhering

**Best Use:** Lightweight cotton tees, fashion apparel, athletic wear, eco-conscious brands, and designs where soft hand is non-negotiable.

### Discharge Ink – Soft Prints on Dark Cotton

**Overview:** Discharge ink removes (bleaches) the shirt's dye and replaces it with pigment. The result is a print that becomes part of the fabric—no film, maximum softness.

**Chemistry:**
- Water-based carrier with a chemical discharge agent (typically zinc formaldehyde sulfoxylate, ZFS)
- When heated to 230–260°F, the ZFS activates and removes the shirt dye
- Pigment then bonds to the exposed fabric

**Properties:**
- Works only on 100% cotton or high-cotton blends dyed with reactive dyes
- Creates prints that feel and wash exactly like the bare garment (zero hand buildup)
- Produces a vintage, worn-in aesthetic
- Cannot hold fine halftones; best for loose, bold graphics

**Pros:**
- Softest possible hand feel on darks
- Prints are breathable and permanent
- Works on preshrunk, vintage, or secondhand garments (reactive dyes only)
- Ideal for high-fashion and premium labels

**Cons:**
- Limited to 100% cotton or high-percentage cotton blends
- Fails on polyester, rayon, and pigment-dyed fabrics
- Requires 230–260°F minimum; tight temperature control
- Unpredictable results; color shift depends on the original shirt dye
- Cannot reproduce solid whites (removes dye to the garment's natural color)
- Design detail is limited; halftones break apart
- Longer cure window than plastisol

**Best Use:** Vintage-style drops, luxury cotton basics, soft-hand graphics where the garment feel is the product.

### Specialty Inks – Puff, Gel, Shimmer, High-Density, Glow, Reflective

**Puff Ink / Puff Additive:**
- Mixed at 10–20% additive-to-plastisol by weight
- Activates at 200°F; full rise at 260°F
- Critical: Do not exceed 280–290°F or the puff collapses
- Cure: 260–270°F in a dedicated puff oven (lower than standard plastisol)
- Creates raised, 3D effect; commonly used on hoodies and streetwear
- Mesh: 86–110 to maintain deposit for rise

**Gel Ink:**
- Translucent plastisol; allows garment color to show through
- Creates a layered, watercolor-like effect
- Cure: 320°F (standard plastisol)
- Mesh: 110–156
- Popular for vintage and distressed prints

**Shimmer / Metallic Ink:**
- Plastisol base with mica or metal flake particles
- Reflects light; adds sparkle to graphics
- Mesh: 110–156 (larger particles require lower mesh)
- Cure: 320°F
- Can clog finer mesh; avoid 200+ unless specifically formulated for halftone shimmer

**High-Density Ink:**
- Thick plastisol formulation; deposits heavy ink with texture
- Mesh: 65–110
- Creates bold, raised prints
- Cure: 320°F
- Popular for athletic and streetwear

**Glow-in-the-Dark Ink:**
- Contains phosphorescent particles; glows under UV or in darkness
- Cure: 320°F (standard plastisol)
- Mesh: 110–156
- Brightness fades over wash cycles; re-charge under UV or bright light
- Often used as a top color with white underbase underneath

**Reflective Ink:**
- Contains reflective particles (glass beads or prismatic materials)
- Reflects headlights and bright light
- Mesh: 110–156
- Cure: 320°F
- Common in safety apparel and high-visibility designs

### Low-Cure Plastisol for Polyester & Performance Garments

**Why Low-Cure Matters:**
Most polyester and poly-blend fabrics are dyed with heat-sensitive dyes that sublimate (turn to gas and release color) at 260–280°F. Standard 320°F plastisol cure activates the garment dye, causing it to migrate up into the ink layer—a phenomenon called "dye migration" or "color ghosting."

**Low-Cure Plastisol Specs:**
- Cure temperature: 260–280°F (vs. 320°F standard)
- Dwell time: 60–90 seconds at target temperature
- Formulation: Modified PVC resin with lower-activation-point plasticizer
- Color selection: Slightly smaller range than standard plastisol, but expanding
- Cost: 10–20% more than standard plastisol

**Dye Migration Prevention:**
1. Use low-cure plastisol (260–280°F) on polyester to stay below sublimation point
2. Use barrier/blocker inks (white underbase) to insulate color from garment dye
3. For performance wear (moisture-wicking, stretch), consider silicone-based or hybrid inks (more flexible, naturally resistant to dye migration)
4. Test cure temperature and time on sample garments; document the sweet spot for each fabric supplier

**Blended Fabrics (50/50 Cotton-Poly):**
- More forgiving than 100% poly but still prone to dye migration
- Use 300°F cure as a compromise, or step up to low-cure plastisol for safety
- Tri-blends (cotton-poly-rayon) are the most unpredictable; pre-test every dye lot

## Mesh Count Fundamentals

Mesh count is the number of wires per linear inch (wpi) in the screen's fabric. It directly controls ink deposit, dot definition, and halftone quality.

### Standard Mesh Count Ranges & Use Cases

| Mesh Count | Wires/Inch | Thread Type | Primary Use | Ink Type | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **65–86** | 65–86 | Hi-TEX (standard) | Underbase, athletic numbers, high-deposit specialty | Plastisol, puff, gel, high-density | Maximum ink deposit; coarse detail; thick hand |
| **110** | 110 | Hi-TEX | Specialty inks, underbase, coverage on darks | Plastisol, puff, gel | Good balance of deposit and detail |
| **125** | 125 | Hi-TEX | Underbase, athletic numbers, thick-ink applications | Plastisol | Slightly finer than 110; still high deposit |
| **156** | 156 | Hi-TEX | White underbase, general-purpose on darks | Plastisol, water-based | Industry standard underbase; good opacity |
| **200** | 200 | Hi-TEX | Mid-range color work, simulated-process | Plastisol, water-based | Sweet spot for balance |
| **230** | 230 | Hi-DRO (thin-thread S-mesh) | Detail, halftones, typical sim-process top colors | Plastisol, water-based | Finer dot reproduction |
| **305** | 305 | Hi-DRO | Highlight white, fine halftones, thin lines | Plastisol, water-based | Very fine detail; low deposit |
| **355–400+** | 355–400 | Hi-DRO | Ultra-fine halftone detail, watercolor effects | Water-based, thin plastisol | Extreme fineness; requires perfect setup |

### Thread Type: Hi-TEX vs. Hi-DRO (S-Mesh)

**Hi-TEX (Standard Thread):**
- Thicker thread profile (~0.006 inch diameter)
- Smaller openings between threads
- Greater deposit of ink
- Best for: plastisol, thick inks, coarser detail, colors and underlayers
- Dominant in traditional plastisol shops
- More forgiving of squeegee pressure and off-contact variation

**Hi-DRO / S-Mesh (Thin Thread):**
- Thinner thread profile (~0.004 inch diameter)
- Larger openings; more open area per mesh count
- Finer halftone dot reproduction; less dot gain
- Best for: water-based inks, fine halftones, highlight whites, fine lines
- Superior for simulated-process (sim-process) color separations
- Requires tighter press setup and tension discipline
- Also called "DRO" or "High-Open S-mesh"

**Why S-Mesh Holds Halftones Better:** The thinner threads create sharper, cleaner dot edges, reducing dot gain (the tendency for halftone dots to grow during printing). A 230 S-mesh can hold a 45–50 LPI halftone cleanly; a 230 Hi-TEX mesh often struggles above 40 LPI.

### Ink Deposit & Coverage

Lower mesh counts deposit more ink; higher mesh counts deposit less ink but provide finer detail. Typical ink film thickness by mesh count (plastisol on cotton):

- 65–86 mesh: 3–4 mils (very heavy; used for bold, opaque coverage)
- 110–156 mesh: 2–3 mils (standard for underbase and mid-tone work)
- 200–230 mesh: 1–2 mils (lighter; used for halftones and detail)
- 305+ mesh: 0.5–1 mil (ultra-thin; used for fine halftones and watercolor effects)

**Practical Rule:** If you're printing a white underbase on a dark garment, use 156 mesh to ensure opacity. If you're printing a light halftone highlight, use 305 mesh to keep the print soft and thin.

## Mesh-LPI-Halftone Relationship

The relationship between mesh count and halftone LPI (lines per inch) is critical for reproducing detail and avoiding dot gain (unwanted dot expansion that destroys halftones).

### The Rule of Thumb

**Conservative (Safe):** LPI ≤ mesh count ÷ 5
- 200 mesh ÷ 5 = 40 LPI max
- 305 mesh ÷ 5 = 61 LPI max
- 156 mesh ÷ 5 = 31 LPI max

**Aggressive (Requires Tight Setup):** LPI ≤ mesh count ÷ 4
- 200 mesh ÷ 4 = 50 LPI
- 305 mesh ÷ 4 = 76 LPI
- 156 mesh ÷ 4 = 39 LPI

**Middle Ground (4.5 Factor):**
- 200 mesh ÷ 4.5 = 44 LPI
- 305 mesh ÷ 4.5 = 68 LPI

### Why This Matters

If you violate the rule (e.g., printing 60 LPI through a 200 mesh), the halftone dots are so large relative to the mesh openings that:
1. Multiple threads pass through each dot, causing jagged edges
2. Squeegee pressure causes dots to grow beyond their original size (dot gain)
3. Midtones and shadows fill in; the halftone appears muddy
4. Fine detail is lost

Conversely, if your LPI is too low for the mesh count (e.g., 20 LPI on 305 mesh), the dots are small relative to the openings, and ink floods through, creating an excessively soft, undefined print.

### For Simulated-Process (Photo-Real) Printing

Most sim-process shops work in these ranges:
- **Light base (white underbase):** 156–200 mesh, 35–45 LPI
- **Mid-tone colors:** 200 mesh, 40–50 LPI
- **Highlight / shadows:** 230–305 mesh, 50–70 LPI
- **Extreme detail (fine lines, type):** 355+ mesh, 65–85 LPI

## Underbase Theory & Practice

An underbase is a layer of opaque ink (almost always white) printed first on dark garments to provide a foundation so that color inks appear vibrant and true. Without an underbase, dark fabric colors bleed through and desaturate the print.

### Why Underbase Is Essential on Dark Garments

**Color Opacity Problem:** Most plastisol and water-based inks are semi-transparent. When applied to a black or navy shirt, the garment color shows through the ink, shifting hue and reducing saturation. A white underbase blocks the garment color and ensures the top colors sit on a white background.

**The Layering Effect:**
- Dark garment (black)
- White underbase (blocks the black)
- Top color (sits on white; appears vibrant)

Without this sandwich, the top color is dull: red becomes maroon, yellow becomes olive, etc.

### Underbase Mesh Selection

**Standard Underbase Mesh:** 156–200
- 156 mesh: Maximum opacity; heavy hand; used when coverage is paramount (dark navy, heather)
- 200 mesh: Balance of opacity and hand feel; versatile
- Avoid 230+ for underbase; insufficient deposit for full coverage

**Why Not Finer:** A 230 or 305 mesh underbase will not deposit enough ink to fully block the garment color, especially on dark fabrics or high-contrast prints.

### Underbase Choke (Shrinking)

The underbase must be **smaller than the top colors** by 1–2 pixels (or ~1 point in print-speak). This is called "choke" and serves two purposes:

1. **Prevents underbase from showing around the edges** of the top colors
2. **Prevents registration misalignment** from exposing the white underbase at color boundaries

**How to Choke in Artwork:**
- If your top color is 100x100 pixels, shrink the underbase to 98x98 pixels (1 pixel per side)
- For larger designs, use 2 pixels per side
- If printing on 156 mesh, the choke can be tighter (1 pixel); on 110 mesh, use 1.5–2 pixels

**Critical:** Choke the underbase from all sides equally. Do not choke one edge and not another, or the misalignment will be obvious.

### Flash and Print Sequence

After printing the white underbase, you must **flash cure** it before printing top colors.

**Why Flash Is Necessary:**
- The underbase is wet plastisol (or water-based ink in gel state)
- Printing directly on top before drying causes the colors to mix, muddying the underbase
- A brief flash (1–3 seconds at 350–400°F on the surface) tacks the underbase dry enough to accept the next color without bleeding

**Flash Timing:**
- Bring the ink to ~140–160°F (the gel state, where it's no longer wet but not fully cured)
- This is called "flash curing" or "gel curing"
- Do not fully cure the underbase (that comes at the end of the job)

### Wet-on-Wet vs. Wet-on-Dry Inking

**Wet-on-Wet:** Printing a color directly onto a wet (just-printed) underbase without flashing. This causes:
- Color bleeding into the underbase
- Muddy, desaturated prints
- Loss of registration and crispness
- **Avoid unless it's a very specific effect you want (e.g., watercolor or bleed-together look)**

**Wet-on-Dry (Flash Between):** Flashing the underbase to gel state before printing top colors. This ensures:
- Clean separation between underbase and top colors
- Sharp, crisp registration
- True color saturation
- **This is the standard production method**

### Underbase ≠ White Highlight

**Underbase:**
- Printed first; foundation layer
- Must be opaque; covers the garment color
- Is "hidden" behind top colors; not visible in the final print
- Typically less detailed (can be a choke version of the design)
- Mesh: 156–200

**White Highlight:**
- Printed last; final layer
- Adds visual pop and brightness
- Visible in the final print
- Can be finely detailed (small type, fine lines)
- Mesh: 230–305

**Key Difference:** The underbase creates opacity so top colors appear bright. The highlight is the final white layer that sits on top of colors to add pop and definition. Many beginner printers confuse these; they serve entirely different functions.

**Example: Dark Navy Tee with Red Logo and White Trim**
1. Print white underbase (156 mesh) — full logo shape, slightly choked
2. Flash to gel
3. Print red color (200 mesh) — full logo, properly registered on underbase
4. Flash to gel
5. Print white highlight (305 mesh) — only the trim/outline; adds final pop

## Print Order Recipes

The sequence in which you print colors matters. Wet ink mixes with wet ink; dry (flashed) ink does not.

### Recipe 1: Two-Color on Dark Garment (Underbase Required)

**Design:** Black tee, red and yellow logo

1. **Screen 1 – White Underbase** (156 mesh, plastisol)
   - Mesh: 156 Hi-TEX
   - Ink: Plastisol white
   - Choke: Shrink 1–2 pixels from logo bounds
   - Print: Full pass; moderate to firm pressure
   - **FLASH:** 2–3 seconds at 350°F surface temp

2. **Screen 2 – Red Color** (200 mesh, plastisol)
   - Mesh: 200 S-mesh or Hi-TEX
   - Ink: Plastisol red (transparent, allowing white to show if thin)
   - Print: Full pass; moderate pressure
   - Registration: Align to underbase (pin or floating register)
   - **FLASH:** 1–2 seconds at 350°F (gel cure, not full cure)

3. **Screen 3 – Yellow Color** (200 mesh, plastisol)
   - Mesh: 200 S-mesh
   - Ink: Plastisol yellow
   - Print: Full pass
   - Registration: Align to underbase and red (not directly on red)
   - **NO FLASH** (yellow is the last color; goes to cure)

4. **Final Cure:** Push through dryer at 320°F, 30–60 seconds belt time (adjust for belt speed)

---

### Recipe 2: Simulated-Process on Dark Garment (Photo-Real)

**Design:** Photoreal image on black tee, 6-color sim-process

1. **Screen 1 – White Base / Light Underbase** (156 mesh, plastisol white)
   - Covers entire image area
   - Choke from all sides
   - **FLASH:** 2–3 seconds

2. **Screen 2 – Highlight White / Light Grey** (200 mesh, S-mesh, plastisol)
   - Lightest tones of the image; 20–30 LPI halftone
   - Printed on white underbase
   - **FLASH:** 1–2 seconds

3. **Screen 3 – Light Flesh / Tan** (200 mesh, S-mesh, plastisol, 40 LPI)
   - Mid-light tones
   - **FLASH:** 1–2 seconds

4. **Screen 4 – Medium Tone / Brown** (230 mesh, S-mesh, plastisol, 50 LPI)
   - Mid-range density
   - **FLASH:** 1–2 seconds

5. **Screen 5 – Dark Brown / Shadow** (230 mesh, S-mesh, plastisol, 50 LPI)
   - Darker tones; begins to add depth
   - **FLASH:** 1–2 seconds

6. **Screen 6 – Black / Darkest Shadows** (305 mesh, S-mesh, plastisol, 65 LPI)
   - Finest detail; sharpens edges
   - This is the final color; **NO FLASH**

7. **Final Cure:** 320°F, 45–60 seconds

**Why This Order?** Light to dark prevents dark ink from contaminating light tones. Each color flash-cures to prevent wet-on-wet bleeding. The darkest screen (black) is last so it sits on top and sharpens the overall image.

---

### Recipe 3: Water-Based on Light Garment (No Underbase)

**Design:** Navy design on white tee

1. **Screen 1 – Navy Color** (200 mesh, S-mesh, water-based, 40 LPI halftone)
   - Highest opacity water-based ink
   - No underbase needed (white garment provides contrast)
   - Print: Full pass; moderate squeegee pressure
   - **NO FLASH** (water-based dries by air evaporation; flash would over-cure and cause adhesion loss)
   - Allow 5–10 minutes air dry or forced-air dry at 160–180°F for 2–3 minutes

2. **Cure:** Either air dry overnight or 3–5 minutes forced-air at 160–180°F

**Note:** Water-based on light garments is straightforward; no underbase is needed. The white background provides contrast. Use 200–230 mesh S-mesh to avoid flooding and to maintain halftone integrity.

---

### Recipe 4: Discharge Ink on Dark Cotton (Vintage Soft-Hand)

**Design:** Vintage-style graphic, loose vector art, dark heather grey 100% cotton tee

1. **Screen 1 – Discharge Ink** (110 mesh, Hi-TEX, discharge ink)
   - Design should be bold and loose (avoid fine detail)
   - No underbase; discharge works directly on garment
   - Print: Full pass; firm squeegee pressure (discharge is thicker than plastisol)
   - **FLASH / CURE:** 2–3 seconds at 230°F minimum, up to 260°F for full dye removal (not a light gel cure; full activation)

2. **Cure:** Must reach 260°F at ink core; 45–60 seconds conveyor belt time

**Why Different Cure?** Discharge requires full temperature to activate the ZFS chemical. Flashing to gel state (like plastisol) won't remove the dye. Full cure = full dye removal = truest vintage effect.

---

### Recipe 5: Puff Ink (Raised 3D Effect)

**Design:** Small logo, streetwear aesthetic, light or dark garment (puff works on both)

1. **Screen 1 – White Underbase** (156 mesh, plastisol white) — *optional, only on darks*
   - If printing on dark garment, white underbase helps puff stand out
   - Choke 1–2 pixels
   - **FLASH:** 2 seconds at 350°F

2. **Screen 2 – Puff Ink** (86–110 mesh, Hi-TEX, plastisol + puff additive, 10–20% additive ratio)
   - Puff additive mixed into low-cure or standard plastisol
   - Thicker ink requires lower mesh
   - Print: Firm pressure to deposit heavy ink (puff needs high deposit to rise)
   - **CRITICAL CURE:** 260–270°F in a dedicated low-temp puff oven
   - **DO NOT CURE at 320°F** — the puff will collapse or flatten

3. **Cure:** 260–270°F, 60–90 seconds. Test on scrap first. If puff doesn't rise fully, add 10 seconds. If it collapses, reduce temp to 255°F.

## Registration & Precision

Registration is the alignment of multiple screens to create a single design. Misaligned screens result in color gaps, outlines, and junk prints.

### Pin Register vs. Floating Register

**Pin Register (Recommended for Multi-Color):**
- Screens are mounted on bushings that align to pins on the press pallet
- Bushings lock the screen in a fixed position
- All screens are mounted to bushings; each screen is perfectly aligned
- Allows for tight registration tolerance (±1/64 inch, or 0.015 inch)
- Requires registration marks (alignment guides) on each screen film

**How Pin Register Works:**
1. Artwork is separated into individual color screens
2. Each screen film includes registration marks (crosshairs or corners)
3. During burning, the registration mark is burned into the screen
4. On press, the first screen is aligned and locked to the pallet pins
5. Subsequent screens are aligned to the same pins; they automatically sit in the correct position

**Floating Register (Older Multi-Station Presses):**
- Screens are locked to individual platens or stations
- Operator manually aligns each screen by eye (using a guide print or design beneath the screen)
- Tolerance: ±1/32 to ±1/16 inch (loose)
- Labor-intensive; prone to drift over production
- **Less common in modern shops**

### Registration Tolerance for Simulated-Process

**Standard Tolerance:** ±1/64 inch (0.015 inch, ~0.4 mm)

For a 10x10 inch design, this tolerance is:
- Tight enough that color separation is clean (colors sit exactly where designed)
- Loose enough that normal press operation and fabric movement can accommodate
- Tighter than ±1/64 requires specialty equipment (registration cameras, servo-controlled presses)

**For Halftone Work:**
- ±1/64 is acceptable for standard halftone detail
- Ultra-fine halftones (305+ mesh, 70 LPI) may require ±1/128 inch tolerance (very tight)

### Registration Mark Placement

**Standard Placement:**
- One registration mark (crosshair, T-mark, or corner pin) at each corner of the design bounding box
- Marks should be outside the visible print area by at least 0.25 inches
- Marks should be the same on all screens (same x,y position)

**Why Multiple Marks?**
- Diagonal marks catch rotation (yaw) errors
- Multiple marks improve accuracy more than a single mark

### Pallet Adhesive & Platen Prep

**Pallet Adhesive (for pin register):**
- Double-sided pallet tape holds bushings in place
- 3M 467MP or equivalent repositionable spray adhesive
- Clean pallet surface before applying; dust and debris reduce adhesion
- Adhesive should be tacky but not sticky (if too old, replace)

**Platen Prep (for floating register):**
- Ensure platen is clean and dry
- If printing on irregular garments (seams, collars), flatten them on the platen before registration
- Use a guide print (vinyl or marker) beneath the first screen to show the operator where the screen should sit

## Flash Curing & Between-Color Flashing

Flash curing (also called gel curing or tack curing) is a brief exposure to heat (1–3 seconds) that brings ink to the gel state—dry enough to accept the next color without bleeding, but not fully cured.

### When to Flash

**After Each Color (Multi-Color on Dark):**
1. Underbase → flash
2. Red → flash
3. Yellow → flash
4. Final cure (no flash; this is full cure)

**After Specialty Inks Before Covering:**
- Puff → flash (before printing on top)
- Gel or high-density → flash (if another color goes on top)

**Before Highlight White on Any Dark Print:**
- Base colors → flash
- Highlight white → no flash (this is the final color)

**Not After Water-Based (usually):**
- Water-based inks dry by evaporation; flashing can cause over-cure and adhesion loss
- Allow 5–10 minutes air dry or a brief forced-air pass at 160–180°F, but avoid direct flash heat

### Flash Temperature & Time

**Plastisol Flash:**
- Surface temperature: 350–400°F
- Dwell time: 1–3 seconds (variable by ink thickness and garment conductivity)
- Goal: Ink surface is tacky but not sticky; no shine when you look at it

**Test Method:**
- Touch the flashed ink lightly with your finger (after a split-second cool)
- If it's sticky or wet, it needs more flash
- If it's completely hard and fully cured, it's over-flashed
- Ideal: Ink feels slightly tacky; no ink transfers to your finger

**Conveyor Flash:**
- If using a conveyor dryer with a flash section, set the surface temperature gauge to 350°F and allow 1–2 seconds dwell time
- Belt speed affects dwell time: slower belt = longer exposure = more cure
- Document belt speed and flash time for consistency

### Why Over-Flashing Causes Problems

1. **Over-Curing the Underbase:** If the underbase is fully cured (not just gelled), the next color may not adhere well. Plastisol bonds via a slight tackiness; if the underbase is rock-hard, the new color sits on top rather than adhering.

2. **Brittleness:** Over-flashing can cause ink to become brittle and crack during wear or washing.

3. **Hand Feel:** Over-flashed ink can feel stiffer and less flexible.

4. **Color Shift:** Some inks change color or fade slightly if over-cured.

**Sweet Spot:** Flash just enough to gel the underbase; full cure comes at the end.

## Garment Fabric Effects

Fabric content, weave, and dye structure dramatically affect how ink adheres, cures, and washes.

### 100% Cotton – Plastisol's Best Friend

**Advantages:**
- Most receptive to plastisol; ink adheres with minimal process tuning
- Absorbent; ink has grip on the fibers
- Discharge ink works reliably (if dyed with reactive dyes)
- Breathable; soft hand achievable
- Industry standard for t-shirt printing

**Considerations:**
- Preshrunk cotton is standard (account for residual shrinkage ~2–3% from manufacturing)
- 100% cotton is heavier and softer than poly-blends; feels premium
- Natural color variation (raw cotton vs. bleached); ask suppliers for consistency
- Dye quality varies by supplier; discharge results depend on dye lot

**Best For:** Plastisol, discharge, water-based inks; any design style.

### 50/50 Cotton-Polyester Blend

**Challenges:**
- Dye migration risk: The polyester portion is dyed with heat-sensitive dyes that can sublimate at 300°F+, causing colors to shift or "ghost" into the ink
- More difficult to achieve soft hand due to synthetic fibers

**Considerations:**
- Not ideal for low-opacity designs (color shift shows through)
- Underbase / white blocker helps insulate the design from dye migration
- Use 300°F cure temperature as a compromise between standard plastisol (320°F) and low-cure (270°F)
- Or switch to low-cure plastisol (260–280°F) for safety

**Best For:** Underlayers (underbase, blocking colors), low-cure plastisol, or bold designs where color shift is acceptable or desired.

### 100% Polyester – Dye Migration Hazard

**Challenges:**
- Most prone to dye migration; polyester dyes sublimate at 260–280°F
- Standard 320°F cure activates garment dyes
- Results: Color ghosting, halo effect (ink appears to bleed into garment)
- Not ideal for water-based inks (they don't adhere well to slick polyester)

**Solutions:**
1. **Use low-cure plastisol** (260–280°F) to stay below sublimation point
2. **Use poly-specific ink** or silicone-based ink (naturally resistant to dye migration)
3. **Use a dye blocker / barrier ink** (white plastisol formulated to block dye migration)
4. **Reduce ink deposit** (use higher mesh, lighter squeegee pressure) to minimize interaction
5. **Use discharge alternative: sublimation printing** (for polyester, this is often more effective than screen printing)

**Best For:** Low-cure plastisol, poly-specific inks, silicone inks, or sublimation transfers (if dye migration is critical).

### Performance / Tri-Blends (Cotton-Poly-Rayon)

**Challenges:**
- Most unpredictable blend; each fiber has different properties
- Dye migration (poly portion), shrinkage (rayon portion), absorbency (cotton portion)
- Striated appearance; heather or variegated colors are common
- Extremely soft hand required; ultra-thin ink layers

**Considerations:**
- Tri-blends are premium garments; customers expect perfect results
- Dye lot variation is higher than 100% cotton
- Rayon fibers are delicate; avoid aggressive squeegee pressure
- Cure temperature is critical; over-curing can scorch rayon fibers

**Solutions:**
1. **Pre-test every dye lot** with sample prints and wash tests
2. **Use low-cure plastisol** (260–280°F)
3. **Use white underbase** to block dye migration and striation
4. **Use soft-hand inks** or water-based to minimize hand feel
5. **Light squeegee pressure** and moderate off-contact to avoid fiber damage

**Best For:** Premium apparel brands, fashion wear; requires process discipline and testing.

### Dark Cotton vs. Light Cotton – Coverage & Choke Considerations

**Dark Cotton (Black, Navy, Heather, Dark Grey):**
- Requires white underbase for any color work
- Mesh for underbase: 156–200 (heavier deposit for opacity)
- Choke: 1–2 pixels (standard)
- Colors appear vibrant when layered on white base
- Discharge is an excellent option for soft hand

**Light Cotton (White, Light Grey, Pale Yellow):**
- No white underbase needed (garment color provides contrast)
- Mesh for colors: 200–230 (can be finer, since no underbase is needed)
- Choke: None (or minimal) — colors can sit edge-to-edge without underbase showing
- Water-based ink works well (contrast is built-in)
- Discharge is not an option (would create blank space, not a visible design)

### Garment Color & Ink Opacity

**Dark Garments Require Highly Opaque Inks:**
- Royal blue shirt: Requires white underbase + opaque top color (e.g., opaque red), not transparent red
- Black shirt: Standard white underbase + color
- Heather grey: May appear speckled (fabric is variated); underbase may not fully cover all speckles

**Heather & Variated Colors:**
- Heather fabrics are blended fibers (light + dark yarn together); small underbase gaps show heather speckles
- Remedy: Increase underbase mesh from 156 to 110–125 to increase deposit, or accept slight variated look
- Some brands feature this as a "vintage" aesthetic

**Light Garments Allow Thinner Ink:**
- White tee with dark design: 1–2 mil ink is adequate (use 200–230 mesh)
- Pale garment with mid-tone color: Slightly heavier deposit may be needed
- Very light colors may show slight color shift; pre-test

## Press Setup & Variables

The physical setup of the press—off-contact, squeegee hardness, angle, and pressure—controls dot gain, print sharpness, and consistency.

### Off-Contact (Screen Height)

Off-contact is the distance between the screen and the pallet surface when the screen is not pressed down. This gap is critical.

**Why Off-Contact Matters:**
- Too little off-contact (screen too close): Ink bleeds under the screen edges; halftones fill in; soft prints
- Too much off-contact (screen too far): Squeegee angle is too steep; insufficient ink transfer; light prints
- Optimal off-contact: Minimal gap that allows halftone dots to print cleanly

**Typical Off-Contact Range:**
- Coarse designs (110 mesh, 20 LPI): 1/8 to 1/4 inch (3–6 mm)
- Mid-range (200 mesh, 40 LPI): 1/16 to 1/8 inch (1.5–3 mm)
- Fine halftones (305 mesh, 65 LPI): 1/32 to 1/16 inch (0.75–1.5 mm)

**How to Set Off-Contact:**
1. Mount the screen on the press
2. Place a spacer shim (plastic or metal, measured thickness) between the screen and pallet
3. Lower the screen onto the shim
4. Remove the shim; the gap it leaves is your off-contact
5. Example: 1/16 inch shim = 1/16 inch off-contact

**Note:** High-tension screens (used in professional shops) support lower off-contact distances (tighter halftones, less dot gain). Low-tension screens (common in smaller shops) require higher off-contact.

### Squeegee Durometer (Hardness)

Squeegee durometers range from 50–90. Lower durometer = softer; higher durometer = harder.

**Common Durometers:**
- 55–60: Very soft; used for coarse screens and thick ink
- 65–70: Standard; most versatile
- 75–80: Hard; used for fine halftones and 4-color process
- 85–90: Very hard; used for ultra-fine detail and specialty applications

**Match Durometer to Mesh:**
- 60–65 durometer: 65–110 mesh
- 70 durometer: 110–200 mesh
- 75–80 durometer: 200–305 mesh (standard for sim-process)
- 80+ durometer: 305+ mesh

**Effect on Dot Gain:**
- Soft squeegee (55–60): Greater ink deposit; larger dots; less detail
- Hard squeegee (80+): Precise ink transfer; tighter dot definition; more detail

**Manual vs. Automatic:**
- Manual presses: Operator controls pressure; softer squeegee is forgiving
- Automatic presses: Servo-controlled pressure; harder squeegee for consistency

### Squeegee Angle

The angle of the squeegee blade relative to the screen affects ink transfer.

**Manual Presses (Hand Printing):**
- 20–30 degrees off vertical (toward the operator)
- This angle allows the squeegee to load ink during the flood stroke and transfer ink during the print stroke
- Gentle angle = better control; steep angle = faster print speed

**Automatic Presses:**
- 7–10 degrees off vertical
- Tight angle for consistent ink transfer and minimal pressure
- Any angle adjustment should be in 1–2 degree increments

**Why Angle Matters:**
- Too steep (> 45 degrees): Insufficient ink transfer; light, speckled prints
- Too shallow (< 5 degrees): Excessive ink transfer; dot gain and flooding

### Flood Stroke & Print Stroke

**Flood Stroke:**
- The backward pass of the squeegee (before printing)
- Refills the screen stencil with ink
- Should be slow and gentle to avoid flooding through the mesh into the garment
- Squeegee pressure: Light (let the squeegee blade do the work, not force)

**Print Stroke:**
- The forward pass that transfers ink to the garment
- Should be firm and confident
- Squeegee pressure: Moderate to firm (varies by mesh and ink)
- Speed: Moderate (balance between ink transfer and precision)

**Typical Sequence:**
1. Flood stroke (backward, light pressure) — fill screen with ink
2. Lift screen slightly
3. Print stroke (forward, firm pressure) — transfer ink
4. Lift screen off garment
5. Repeat for next garment

**Stroke Count:**
- Most designs print well with a single flood and single print stroke
- Thick inks or high-density effects may require a second flood + print stroke
- Over-stroking (multiple prints without reflood) depletes ink in the stencil; prints become light

### Dot Gain (Halftone Growth)

Dot gain is the phenomenon where halftone dots grow larger during printing, causing halftones to appear muddy or blown out.

**Causes:**
1. **High squeegee pressure** — ink is forced into and under the stencil opening
2. **Soft squeegee** — allows ink to deform the blade, increasing transfer
3. **High off-contact** — ink sits on screen too long; flows laterally
4. **Dull or damaged squeegee blade** — does not cut ink cleanly; ragged edges
5. **Wet underbase** — ink mixes with tackiness, expanding dot size

**How to Minimize Dot Gain:**
1. Use firm (80 durometer) squeegee for halftone work
2. Set off-contact tightly (1/32 to 1/16 inch for fine halftones)
3. Use minimal squeegee pressure (just enough to transfer ink; not crushing the blade)
4. Ensure squeegee blade is sharp and clean (replace after 50–100k impressions)
5. Flash cure underlayers to gel state (not fully cured, but dry) before printing halftones
6. Use high-tension screens (high-mesh screens should have 30+ oz/in² tension)

## Cure, Wash-Fastness & Testing

Proper cure is the final step that ensures ink adheres permanently to the garment and survives washing.

### Plastisol Cure Temperature & Dwell Time

**Standard Plastisol:**
- Target temperature: 320°F (160°C) at the ink core
- Dwell time: 60–90 seconds at target temperature
- Full cure is when the ink core reaches 320°F and stays there long enough for the PVC to fully polymerize

**Low-Cure Plastisol (Polyester):**
- Target temperature: 260–280°F (127–138°C)
- Dwell time: 60–90 seconds

**Rapid-Cure Plastisol:**
- Target temperature: 270–300°F
- Dwell time: 45–60 seconds

**Under-Curing:**
- Symptoms: Ink rubs off when touched dry; washes out partially after first wash
- Cause: Temperature too low or dwell time too short
- Remedy: Increase belt speed (slower = longer exposure) or raise dryer temperature by 10°F

**Over-Curing:**
- Symptoms: Ink becomes brittle; cracks in flex areas; hand feels stiff
- Cause: Temperature too high or dwell time too long
- Remedy: Reduce dryer temperature or increase belt speed (shorter dwell)

**Cure Methods:**
1. **Conveyor Dryer:** Most common; belt speed controls dwell time. 30–60 seconds at 320°F depends on belt speed
2. **Flash Cure Unit:** Manual or automatic; brings screen to 400°F surface, 1–3 seconds
3. **Heated Press:** Less common; can cure small designs
4. **Air Dry (Water-Based):** 24–48 hours ambient + 3–5 minutes at 160–180°F forced-air

### Test Strips (Cure Validation)

A test strip is a piece of garment (scrap cotton tee or muslin fabric) printed with a design and cured in the dryer. It's used to validate that the dryer is curing correctly.

**Standard Test Strip Procedure:**
1. Print a solid color (100% ink coverage) on white cotton scrap
2. Print a sample design with halftones
3. Cure through the dryer at target temperature and speed
4. Allow to cool
5. Rub the ink firmly with your finger — ink should not rub off
6. Stretch the fabric — ink should flex with the fabric (not crack)
7. Wash the test strip (warm water, detergent, 5 min agitation) and air dry
8. Inspect: Ink should remain in place and not fade

**Calibration:**
- If ink rubs off → under-cured (increase dryer temp or slow belt)
- If ink cracks → over-cured (reduce dryer temp or speed up belt)
- If ink color fades after wash → under-cured or low-quality ink

### Wash-Fastness Testing

For production quality, prints should survive commercial laundry conditions.

**Standard Home Wash Test:**
1. Warm water (100–130°F), standard detergent
2. 5–10 minute agitation
3. Air dry (do not machine dry if testing for color bleed; heat can set bleeding)
4. Inspect for fading, cracking, or color bleed

**Commercial / Durability Test:**
1. Hot water (160°F+), heavy-duty detergent
2. 20+ minute cycle
3. Machine dry at medium heat
4. Repeat 10+ times (for promotional wear, test 20+ wash cycles)
5. Inspect for durability, color shift, and cracking

**Success Criteria:**
- No visible fading
- No cracking in flex areas (armpits, neck, hem)
- No color bleed to adjacent areas
- No separation from garment

### Color Shift & Fading

**Plastisol:**
- Should not fade under normal wash conditions
- Color may shift slightly with age and sun exposure (UV fading)
- Remedy: Use UV-stable plastisol for outdoor garments

**Water-Based:**
- More prone to fading than plastisol (especially reds, oranges)
- May lose saturation after 10+ washes
- Remedy: Use premium water-based inks formulated for durability

**Discharge:**
- Fades as the garment's dye fades (not the print itself)
- Over-curing (above 260°F) can scorch the fabric, yellowing it
